Some call this ‘learning things the hard way.’ Some call this life. Now, folks always told me that consulting was either a feast or famine, which sounds straightforward, but it turns out I didn’t really know what this meant until I had to deal with Chase.

    Earlier this year, for reasons that are still not fully known to me, and despite my existing relationship with Chase (dating back to my first job out of university over a decade ago!), Chase froze $180,000 of my money without warning. This left me scrambling to pay employees and nearly derailed my business—all without explanation. It was a major wake-up call not just for me but for any entrepreneur. The importance of diversifying your banking and choosing financial partners that actually support small businesses has never been more important.

    Content Creation Mastery: 9 Strategies to 10x Your Impact

    In today's oversaturated digital landscape, creating content that truly resonates is harder than ever. Whether you're a seasoned marketer or a budding creator, these 9 strategies will elevate your content from forgettable to unforgettable, helping you:

    • Craft titles that stop readers mid-scroll and compel them to click
    • Write intros that hook readers and keep them engaged throughout
    • Build unshakeable credibility with concrete, data-driven evidence
    • Structure content for maximum retention and impact
    • Position yourself as the go-to expert in your field

    Let's dive deep into each strategy and transform your content creation process.

    1. Craft Titles That Demand Attention

    Your title is the gatekeeper of your content. In a world where we're bombarded with information, your title needs to cut through the noise and grab attention instantly.

    Key Elements of Attention-Grabbing Titles:

    1. Evoke Emotion: Tap into your audience's desires, fears, or curiosities. Example: "The Writing Hack That Tripled My Audience Overnight"

    2. Address Pain Points: Highlight a common problem your audience faces. Example: "End 'Writer's Block' Forever: A Foolproof 3-Step System"

    3. Offer Clear Value: Specify what the reader will gain. Example: "5 Persuasion Techniques That Boosted Our Sales by 287%"

    4. Use Numbers: Odd numbers often perform better than even numbers. Example: "7 Unconventional Marketing Tactics Used by Top Brands"

    5. Create Urgency: Encourage immediate action. Example: "Limited Time: Learn the SEO Secret That's Transforming Businesses"

    6. Ask Intriguing Questions: Pique curiosity with thought-provoking queries. Example: "Is Your Content Strategy Secretly Sabotaging Your Growth?"

    Pro Tips for Title Optimization:

    • A/B Testing: Use social media to test different title variations. Post 2-3 versions of your title as tweets or social media updates. After 24 hours, analyze which one got the most engagement (likes, shares, comments) and use the winner for your full piece.

    • Keyword Research: Use tools like Google Keyword Planner or SEMrush to identify high-volume, low-competition keywords relevant to your topic. Incorporate these naturally into your title for SEO benefits.

    • Character Count: Keep titles under 60 characters to ensure they display fully in search engine results.

    • Power Words: Incorporate emotionally charged words like "essential," "proven," or "revolutionary" to increase click-through rates.

    Remember, your title is a promise to your reader. Make sure your content delivers on that promise to build trust and keep readers coming back for more.

    2. Hook with a Powerful Intro

    Once your title has done its job, your introduction needs to seal the deal. A powerful intro validates the reader's decision to click and compels them to keep reading.

    The Anatomy of a Captivating Intro:

    1. Validate the Reader's Challenge: Show that you understand their pain points.
    2. Hint at Your Unique Solution: Tease the value your content will provide.
    3. Establish Your Credibility: Briefly showcase why you're qualified to help.

    Example: "Struggling to stand out in a sea of content? You're not alone. In today's digital noise, capturing and keeping attention feels like an uphill battle. I've been there, watching my carefully crafted posts disappear into the void. But after years of trial and error, and helping 100+ creators cut through the clutter to grow their audiences by 500%+, I've cracked the code. In this guide, I'll share the exact framework that transformed my content from ignored to irresistible. Get ready to turn your readers into raving fans."

    Techniques for Crafting Compelling Intros:

    • Start with a Shocking Statistic: "Did you know that 55% of visitors spend less than 15 seconds on a webpage? In the next 5 minutes, I'll show you how to keep readers glued to your content."

    • Use the "Problem-Agitate-Solve" (PAS) Formula:

    • Problem: Identify the issue your reader is facing.
    • Agitate: Emphasize the consequences of not solving this problem.

    • Solve: Introduce your solution.

    • Tell a Relatable Story: Share a brief anecdote that illustrates the problem and hints at the solution.

    • Ask a Thought-Provoking Question: Engage readers' minds immediately with a question that makes them curious about the answer.

    Remember, your intro should be concise (aim for 3-5 sentences) but powerful enough to convince the reader that the rest of your content is worth their time.

    3. Use Evidence, Not Adjectives

    In a world where anyone can claim expertise, concrete evidence sets you apart and builds unshakeable credibility.

    The Power of Evidence-Based Writing:

    Replace vague claims with specific, verifiable data:

    ❌ "Our platform is blazing fast" ✅ "Our platform delivers 3ms average response time with 99.99% uptime last quarter"

    ❌ "This method significantly increases engagement" ✅ "This method increased our client's Instagram engagement by 317% in 30 days"

    Types of Evidence to Incorporate:

    1. Data and Statistics: Use numbers to quantify results and impact.
    2. Case Studies: Share detailed examples of how your methods worked in real-world scenarios.
    3. Expert Quotes: Cite respected authorities in your field to support your points.
    4. Before and After Comparisons: Visually demonstrate the impact of your strategies.
    5. Social Proof: Include testimonials, user reviews, or social media feedback.

    Guidelines for Effective Evidence Use:

    • Be Specific: Instead of "many users," say "1,237 users."
    • Cite Sources: Always provide references for your data to build trust.
    • Use Visuals: Charts, graphs, and infographics can make data more digestible and impactful.
    • Update Regularly: Ensure your evidence is current and relevant.

    Aim for statements that can be: - Visualized: Paint a clear picture in the reader's mind. - Proven false: Make claims that are specific enough to be verified or debunked. - Said only by you: Offer unique insights or data that sets you apart from competitors.

    By grounding your content in solid evidence, you not only build credibility but also provide tangible value that readers can apply to their own challenges.

    4. Foreshadow Value

    Previewing the key takeaways of your content serves two crucial purposes: it sets clear expectations for what readers will gain, and it creates anticipation that keeps them engaged throughout.

    Effective Value Foreshadowing Techniques:

    1. Numbered Lists: Outline the specific strategies or tips you'll cover. Example: "In this guide, you'll discover 5 proven techniques to double your email open rates."

    2. Outcome Statements: Clearly state what readers will be able to do after consuming your content. Example: "By the end of this article, you'll know how to craft headlines that convert 43% better than the industry average."

    3. Problem-Solution Pairs: List the problems you'll address and hint at your solutions. Example: "Struggling with low engagement? Learn how to implement the 'Skyscraper Technique' to 10x your backlinks and boost your content's visibility."

    4. Time-Based Promises: Set expectations for how quickly readers can see results. Example: "Discover three strategies you can implement today to see a measurable increase in your social media followers within the next week."

    5. Exclusivity Hints: Tease unique insights or strategies that aren't commonly known. Example: "Uncover the little-known content distribution hack that top influencers are using to reach millions – without spending a dime on ads."

    Implementing Value Foreshadowing:

    • Place your value preview near the beginning of your content, typically after the introduction.
    • Use bullet points or a numbered list to make it easily scannable.
    • Be specific and quantifiable where possible.
    • Align your foreshadowed value with your audience's key pain points and desires.

    Example Value Foreshadowing Section:

    "By the time you finish this comprehensive guide, you'll have mastered: - The 'Curiosity Gap' technique to boost email open rates by up to 203% - A step-by-step process for crafting headlines that outperform your competitors by 43% - The secret 'Content Multiplication' method to create 10 pieces of high-engaging content from a single idea - How to leverage AI tools to cut your content creation time in half while doubling output - The exact outreach template that landed us features in Forbes, Entrepreneur, and TechCrunch

    Whether you're a solo content creator or leading a marketing team, these strategies will transform your content from overlooked to unforgettable. Let's dive in and revolutionize your content strategy."

    By effectively foreshadowing the value of your content, you create a roadmap that guides readers through your piece, keeping them engaged and excited about the insights they're about to gain.

    5. Structure for Scanners and Skimmers

    In today's fast-paced digital world, many readers scan content before committing to reading it in full. Structuring your content for easy scanning ensures that even quick skimmers can grasp your main points and find value.

    Key Structuring Techniques:

    1. Short, Punchy Paragraphs:
    2. Aim for 2-3 sentences per paragraph.

    3. Each paragraph should convey a single main idea.

    4. Bullet Points and Numbered Lists:

    5. Break down complex ideas into digestible points.

    6. Use for steps, tips, or multiple related items.

    7. Descriptive Subheadings:

    8. Use H2, H3, H4 tags to create a clear hierarchy.

    9. Make subheadings informative enough to convey main points.

    10. Bold Text for Emphasis:

    11. Highlight key phrases or takeaways.

    12. Don't overuse – aim for 1-2 bold sections per major point.

    13. Strategic White Space:

    14. Use line breaks between sections to give eyes a rest.

    15. Avoid dense blocks of text.

    16. Relevant Images and Infographics:

    17. Break up text with visual elements.

    18. Use to illustrate complex concepts or data.

    19. Pull Quotes:

    20. Highlight impactful statements or key takeaways.

    21. Use sparingly for maximum effect.

    22. Table of Contents:

    23. For longer pieces, include a clickable table of contents.
    24. Helps readers navigate to sections of interest.

    Example of Well-Structured Content:

[Continue with more sections...]

    Pro Tips for Skimmable Content:

    • Use the Inverted Pyramid: Start with the most important information, then provide supporting details.
    • Incorporate "Skim Lines": Begin paragraphs with bolded phrases that summarize the main point.
    • Utilize Callout Boxes: Highlight key takeaways or important quotes in visually distinct boxes.
    • Employ the "One Idea Per Paragraph" Rule: This helps readers quickly find the information they need.

    By structuring your content for easy scanning, you cater to different reading styles and increase the likelihood that your audience will engage with and benefit from your content, even if they don't read every word.

    6. Make It About Them, Not You

    One of the most powerful shifts you can make in your content is to focus relentlessly on your audience. This approach not only engages readers more effectively but also positions your content as a valuable resource rather than a self-promotional piece.

    The Power of "You-Focused" Content:

    ❌ "I increased conversions by 50% using this method" ✅ "You can boost your conversions by 50% with this proven method"

    Strategies for Reader-Centric Writing:

    1. Use "You" Language:
    2. Replace "I" and "we" with "you" and "your" wherever possible.

    3. Example: "We've developed a new tool" becomes "You can now access a powerful new tool"

    4. Address Reader Benefits Directly:

    5. Focus on how your insights or solutions will improve the reader's life or work.

    6. Example: "This strategy will save you 5 hours a week on content creation"

    7. Ask Questions:

    8. Engage readers by prompting them to reflect on their own experiences.

    9. Example: "How much time do you currently spend on social media management?"

    10. Use Scenario-Based Examples:

    11. Paint pictures of relatable situations your readers might face.

    12. Example: "Imagine opening your analytics dashboard to see your traffic has doubled overnight..."

    13. Provide Actionable Takeaways:

    14. End sections with specific steps readers can implement immediately.

    15. Example: "Action Step: Conduct a content audit using this template [link] to identify your top-performing pieces"

    16. Anticipate and Address Objections:

    17. Show that you understand potential doubts or challenges.

    18. Example: "You might be thinking, 'But I don't have time for another marketing tactic.' Here's how this fits into your existing workflow..."

    19. Customize Content for Different Segments:

    20. Acknowledge various reader scenarios or experience levels.
    21. Example: "If you're just starting out... / For more advanced marketers..."

    Before and After Examples:

    Before (Self-Focused): "In this post, I'll share the email marketing strategy that helped me build a list of 100,000 subscribers."

    After (Reader-Focused): "Discover how you can apply this email marketing strategy to build your own list of 100,000 engaged subscribers, even if you're starting from zero."

    Before (Feature-Focused): "Our new tool includes advanced analytics, customizable templates, and integration with 20+ platforms."

    After (Benefit-Focused): "You'll save hours each week and gain deeper insights into your audience with advanced analytics, easily customizable templates, and seamless integration with all your favorite platforms."

    Pro Tip: The "So What?" Test

    After writing a sentence or paragraph, ask yourself, "So what? How does this benefit my reader?" If you can't immediately answer with a clear benefit, revise the content to make it more reader-centric.

    By consistently framing your content from the reader's perspective, you create a more engaging, valuable, and impactful experience that keeps your audience coming back for more.

    7. Be an Oracle: Predict Future Challenges (Continued)

    1. Audience Evolution:
    2. Predict how your audience's needs and behaviors might change over time.

    3. Example: "As millennials move into leadership roles, B2B content will need to adapt. Here's how to evolve your strategy..."

    4. Cross-Industry Insights:

    5. Look for trends in adjacent industries that might impact your field.
    6. Example: "The gamification techniques revolutionizing EdTech will soon transform content marketing. Here's what you need to know..."

    Techniques for Effective Future Prediction:

    1. Data-Driven Forecasting: Use current data trends to make informed predictions about future developments.

    2. Expert Interviews: Consult with industry leaders and innovators to gain insights into upcoming trends.

    3. Scenario Analysis: Present multiple possible futures and strategies for each, showcasing your versatility and foresight.

    4. Historical Pattern Recognition: Analyze past industry shifts to identify cyclical trends or patterns that might repeat.

    5. Weak Signal Analysis: Pay attention to small, seemingly insignificant changes that could snowball into major shifts.

    Example of Oracle-Style Content:

    "While most content creators are mastering short-form video, forward-thinking marketers are already preparing for the next big shift: immersive, interactive content experiences. Here's what you need to know:

    By preparing for these future trends now, you'll position yourself as an industry leader and future-proof your content strategy. Let's dive deeper into each of these predictions and explore actionable steps you can take today."

    By consistently providing forward-thinking insights, you not only solve immediate problems but also prepare your audience for future success, cementing your position as a trusted authority in your field.

    8. One Clear, Compelling Call-to-Action (CTA)

    The effectiveness of your content isn't just about what you say—it's about what you inspire your readers to do. A powerful call-to-action (CTA) can turn passive readers into active participants, customers, or advocates.

    Principles of Effective CTAs:

    1. Singularity: Focus on one primary action per piece of content.
    2. Clarity: Use clear, action-oriented language.
    3. Value Proposition: Clearly state the benefit of taking action.
    4. Urgency: Create a sense of timeliness or scarcity when appropriate.
    5. Visibility: Make your CTA stand out visually.
    6. Relevance: Ensure the CTA aligns with the content and reader's current stage in the customer journey.

    Types of CTAs:

    1. Content Upgrade: Offer additional, related content. Example: "Download our free 'Content Strategy Blueprint' to implement these strategies today."

    2. Free Trial or Demo: Encourage product exploration. Example: "Start your 14-day free trial now and see your engagement soar."

    3. Consultation or Assessment: Offer personalized advice. Example: "Book your free 30-minute content strategy session with our experts."

    4. Event Registration: Promote webinars, workshops, or conferences. Example: "Reserve your spot for our exclusive 'Content Mastery' webinar."

    5. Community Join: Invite readers to become part of your community. Example: "Join our private 'Content Creators' Slack group for daily tips and networking."

    6. Social Sharing: Encourage content distribution. Example: "Found this useful? Share it with your network to help others level up their content game."

    7. Purchase: Direct sales for products or services. Example: "Unlock all premium features now and save 20% with code CONTENTPRO."

    CTA Best Practices:

    1. A/B Testing: Experiment with different CTA placements, colors, and copy to optimize conversion rates.

    2. Mobile Optimization: Ensure your CTA is easily clickable on mobile devices.

    3. Contextual Relevance: Tailor your CTA to the specific content and where the reader is in their journey.

    4. Use Power Words: Incorporate action-oriented, emotionally charged words like "Exclusive," "Limited," "Now," "Free," "Instant."

    5. Create Contrast: Make your CTA button or link stand out with contrasting colors or design elements.

    6. Reduce Friction: Minimize the steps required to complete the action. One-click signups or auto-filled forms can increase conversion rates.

    7. Provide Social Proof: Include testimonials or user counts near your CTA to build trust.

    Example CTA Variations:

    Basic: "Sign up for our newsletter." Improved: "Join 50,000+ content pros getting weekly insider tips. Sign up now!"

    Basic: "Try our tool." Improved: "Start creating viral content in 5 minutes. Begin your free trial now!"

    Basic: "Contact us for more information." Improved: "Unlock your personalized content strategy. Book your free 30-minute consultation today!"

    Remember, the key is to make your CTA irresistible by clearly communicating the value your reader will receive by taking action. Always align your CTA with the content's purpose and your overall business goals.

    9. Iterate and Improve Based on Data

    Creating great content is an iterative process. By consistently analyzing performance data and refining your approach, you can continuously improve your content's impact and reach.

    Key Metrics to Track:

    1. Engagement Metrics:
    2. Time on page
    3. Scroll depth
    4. Social shares
    5. Comments

    6. Click-through rates (CTR)

    7. Conversion Metrics:

    8. Email sign-ups
    9. Lead magnet downloads
    10. Free trial starts

    11. Product purchases

    12. SEO Metrics:

    13. Organic traffic
    14. Keyword rankings

    15. Backlinks acquired

    16. Content-Specific Metrics:

    17. Video watch time
    18. Podcast listen-through rate
    19. Infographic embeds

    Data-Driven Improvement Strategies:

    1. Analyze Top Performers:
    2. Identify your 3-5 best-performing pieces across key metrics.
    3. Break down what makes them successful (topic, format, promotion strategy, etc.).

    4. Apply these insights to future content creation.

    5. Conduct A/B Tests:

    6. Test different content elements:
      • Headlines
      • Intro paragraphs
      • Content length
      • Visual elements
      • CTAs

    7. Implement winning variations across your content.

    8. Audience Feedback Loop:

    9. Actively engage with comments and social media responses.
    10. Conduct surveys or polls to understand audience preferences.

    11. Use feedback to inform future content topics and formats.

    12. Content Refresh Strategy:

    13. Regularly update high-performing older content with new information.

    14. Repurpose successful content into different formats (e.g., turn a popular blog post into a video or infographic).

    15. Funnel Analysis:

    16. Map out your content funnel from awareness to conversion.

    17. Identify drop-off points and create targeted content to address these gaps.

    18. Competitive Analysis:

    19. Regularly audit competitor content strategies.

    20. Identify gaps in the market that you can fill with unique content.

    21. SEO Optimization Cycle:

    22. Use tools like Google Search Console to identify underperforming keywords.
    23. Optimize existing content for these keywords to improve rankings.

    Implementing a Data-Driven Content Strategy:

    1. Set Clear Goals: Define specific, measurable objectives for each piece of content.

    2. Choose Key Metrics: Select 3-5 core metrics that align with your goals.

    3. Establish Benchmarks: Set performance targets based on industry standards and your historical data.

    4. Regular Reporting: Create a weekly or monthly dashboard to track content performance.

    5. Analysis Sessions: Schedule regular team meetings to review data and brainstorm improvements.

    6. Experiment and Document: Try new approaches, document your hypotheses, and record results.

    7. Continuous Learning: Stay updated on industry trends and algorithm changes that might affect content performance.

    Example: Data-Driven Content Iteration

    Scenario: Your blog post "10 Email Marketing Tips" is performing well but not meeting all goals.

    Data Analysis: - High traffic but low time on page - Good email sign-up rate but low social shares - Strong performance for "email marketing strategies" keyword

    Iteration Plan: 1. Expand content with more actionable tips to increase time on page. 2. Add click-to-tweet quotes to boost social sharing. 3. Create a downloadable email template to improve sign-up conversions. 4. Optimize for related keywords to capture more search traffic. 5. Repurpose content into a video tutorial for multi-channel engagement.

    By implementing these changes and continuing to monitor performance, you can iteratively improve your content's effectiveness and ROI.

    Remember, content creation is both an art and a science. While creativity and intuition play crucial roles, data-driven decision-making ensures that your efforts consistently yield measurable results and drive business growth.

    The RAG Playbook

    When it comes to building and improving Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems, too many teams focus on the wrong things. They obsess over generation before nailing search, implement RAG without understanding user needs, or get lost in complex improvements without clear metrics. I've seen this pattern repeat across startups of all sizes and industries.

    But it doesn't have to be this way. After years of building recommendation systems, instrumenting them, and more recently consulting on RAG applications, I've developed a systematic approach that works. It's not just about what to do, but understanding why each step matters in the broader context of your business.

    Here's the flywheel I use to continually infer and improve RAG systems:

    1. Initial Implementation
    2. Synthetic Data Generation
    3. Fast Evaluations
    4. Real-World Data Collection
    5. Classification and Analysis
    6. System Improvements
    7. Production Monitoring
    8. User Feedback Integration

    How I want you to write

    I'm gonna write something technical.

    It's often less about the nitty-gritty details of the tech stuff and more about learning something new or getting a solution handed to me on a silver platter.

    Look, when I read, I want something out of it. So when I write, I gotta remember that my readers want something too. This whole piece? It's about cluing in anyone who writes for me, or wants me to write for them, on how I see this whole writing product thing.

    I'm gonna lay out a checklist of stuff I'd like to have. It'll make the whole writing gig a bit smoother, you know?

    Crafting Compelling Titles

    I often come across titles like "How to do X with Y,Z technology." These don't excite me because X or Y are usually unfamiliar unless they're already well-known. Its rarely the dream to use X unless X is the dream.

    My dream isn’t to use instructor, its to do something valueble with the data it extracts

    An effective title should:

    • Evoke an emotional response
    • Highlight someone's goal
    • Offer a dream or aspiration
    • Challenge or comment on a belief
    • Address someone's problems

    I believe it's more impactful to write about specific problems. If this approach works, you can replicate it across various scenarios rather than staying too general.

    • Time management for everyone can be a 15$ ebook
    • Time management for executives is a 2000$ workshop

    Aim for titles that answer questions you think everyone is asking, or address thoughts people have but can't quite articulate.

    Instead of "How I do something" or "How to do something," frame it from the reader's perspective with "How you can do something." This makes the title more engaging. Just make sure the difference is advisory if the content is subjective. “How I made a million dollars” might be more reasonable than “How to make a million dollars” since you are the subject and the goal might be to share your story in hopes of helping others.

    This approach ultimately trains the reader to have a stronger emotional connection to your content.

    • "How I do X"
    • "How You Can do X"

    Between these two titles, it's obvious which one resonates more emotionally.

    You can take it further by adding specific conditions. For instance, you could target a particular audience or set a timeframe:

    • How to set up Braintrust
    • How to set up Braintrust in 5 minutes

    NO adjectiives

    I want you to almost always avoid adjectives and try to use evidence instead. Instead of saying "production ready," you can write something like "scaling this to 100 servers or 1 million documents per second." Numbers like that will tell you exactly what the specificity of your product is. If you have to use adjectives rather than evidence, you are probably making something up.

    There's no reason to say something like "blazingly fast" unless those things are already known phrases.

    Instead, say "200 times faster" or "30% faster." A 30% improvement in recommendation system speed is insane.

    There's a 200 times performance improvement because we went from one programming language to another. It's just something that's a little bit more expected and understandable.

    Another test that I really like using recently is tracking whether or not the statements you make can be:

    • Visualized
    • Proven false
    • Said only by you

    If you can nail all three, the claim you make will be more likely to resonate with an audience because only you can say it.

    Earlier this year, I had an example where I embedded all of Wikipedia in 17 minutes with 20 bucks, and it got half a million views. All we posted was a video of me kicking off the job, and then you can see all the log lines go through. You see the number of containers go from 1 out of 50 to 50 out of 50.

    It was easy to visualize and could have been proven false by being unreproducible. Lastly, Modal is the only company that could do that in such an effortless way, which made it unique.

    Strong Introduction

    So, if you end up doing any kind of sales, you'll realize that.

    What you actually need to understand is not what you have to offer as the product, but the size of the pain that the prospect is going through.

    • There are going to be readers that are just kind of curious and bored. They're not really going to be the ones that care about the product itself unless you can contextualize the pain for them.
    • It's really important to have an introduction that contextualizes the pain and foreshadows the solution.

    If we can build that trust and I can correctly describe the pain that you are going through, then you will believe me when I am predicting the pain that you may also go through in the future. Ultimately, that is how you become a leader in the space—by demonstrating your ability to be right consistently.

    The next time you publish or write something, they will believe it, and they will believe that they get value from it.

    Strong Hooks

    In the same sense that a title should often try to change the "how I" to a "how you" by eliciting an emotional response, the introduction can also help select the reader into a group that is feeling the pain.

    This is the same reason why a plumber will have an introduction that says, "Do you have a leaky faucet? Call 1-800-PLUMBERS." That's a much more selective hook than just "I'm the best plumber in town." You can say that to everybody, whereas if someone answers the question of whether they have a leaky faucet, it automatically selects them to be a part of their readership.

    I truly believe if you try to build a product too soon for everybody, you're gonna end up in a bad place.

    Foreshadow Content

    Once you hook them, you still have to first retain them. You can do that by foreshadowing the content you'll cover and even hinting at the reward.

    For example, an introduction could look like the following:

    • If you're making $10,000 a month consulting right now, my goal at the end of this blog post is to help you increase your prices by:
      • Asking the right questions so you understand the value of the solution you're offering.
      • Providing tips on writing proposals and offering different options you can let your customer pay you more.
      • Lastly, sharing some anecdotes with you on how I became more comfortable with charging two or three times more than I did when I started

    Here, I've pre-qualified the reader for a certain range and told them what their goal is by the end of the post.

    Two things are just the tips and the questions I'm going to suggest, and then the final reward is something a little bit more personal. Ideally, they read the first two knowing that my personal stories are coming after. That intro itself outlines the entire post.

    Use Lists

    Once you've hooked your audience, you have to retain them and reward them.

    You'll also see that as part of the foreshadowing content, I've listed three items that I want them to take away. I can also be specific with the number of questions and the number of tips I'm providing.

    By using lists and counting things, I can give them a notion of progress towards the final conclusion. If I'm on the second part, I know where I am in the story. The list itself allows us to break down the body and give the reader a sense of position. Since they know where they are, they know where they're going to be.

    Demonstrate being an Oracle

    One thing that's also really valuable to call out is the fact that you want to be seen as a leader or an oracle to this audience.

    For example, if we go back to this charging more case, it's one thing to demonstrate that you understand that the reader's dream is to be able to charge more. It's useful that you're giving them a couple of tips and stories. But what can be even more powerful is to simultaneously:

    • Demonstrate your knowledge of the current problem.
    • Predict future problems as they come.
    • Foreshadow or reference those future issues in later content.

    For example, the things you do to go from $10,000 to $30,000 a month are very different from what you would need to do to get to $200,000 a month. They require things like hiring, prioritizing your services, and improving distribution. If you can foreshadow that and call that out, when your audience gets to those levels, there's a chance they will remember what you said.

    They'll think, "Wow, Jason was not only right about where I was but also where I was going." This brings a tremendous sense of trust and value.

    It's not just this idea of future work or future considerations of the current work, but actually being able to predict the problems they're going to have in the future and suggesting that you are also the solution to those problems. You can foreshadow that as part of a series or whatever, but the general idea remains.

    Have a Strong CTA

    You also have to think about what the reward for yourself is. Ultimately, you should be writing this because you think the message is important and that you believe your audience should and deserves to get this message. The content of this post delivers the reward to those who stay and finish the article.

    But at the end, you also have to get something in return. You should ask your user to do something. If it's a tweet, a simple one could be a repost, a like, or a share. It could even be a follow. It could be entering some GitHub link and giving the repost a star, etc.

    What I realized to be very important was to make sure you only ask for one thing and don't split the attention. If you do that, you can have solid metrics on how you phrase CTAs and how that converts to certain content. For example:

    • How many people from the tweet go to the blog post?
    • How many go from the blog post to a subscription to a newsletter?

    The more I think about it, the more I believe that most people should be capturing information into a newsletter rather than just Twitter. A direct email is so much more powerful.

    No matter what it is, make sure you only ask for one thing. Sometimes it's to sign up, sometimes it's to try a one-click deploy. But if there's no action that your users can take, you've definitely made a mistake.

    It's also good to call out that taking the action should have some kind of outcome as well.

    • If you want to see more of this content, follow me because I post twice a week.
    • That would be an example that I can qualify as a user and set expectations on what the outcome is.
    • If you like it, then subscribe. You'll get two posts a week.

    I also think in many situations we should have tiers of qualified CTUs.

    If you want to split your traffic, you should have an obvious condition as to which one someone should take. For example, on Indexify:

    • If you're dealing with terabyte-scale datasets, contact us.
    • If you want to try the Open Source library

    It sets a prequalifier.

    The terabyte-scale dataset is an evidence-based pre-filter. It could have said "in production," but something so specific like a terabyte or a terabyte a day really qualifies who should contact indexify

    Living My Best Life: A $20 Million Daydream

    I've been playing a little thought experiment lately: If I had $20 million in the bank, how would I want to live my life? It's not about the money per se, but about imagining a life where financial constraints aren't the primary driver of my decisions. Here's what I've come up with.

    Work: Finding the Sweet Spot

    With financial freedom, I'd aim for a work schedule that balances productivity and passion:

    • 2-3 days a week of focused work
    • Flexibility to pursue projects I'm truly excited about
    • Time for creative exploration and learning

    The goal isn't to stop working, but to work on things that truly matter to me, still enjoy learning and teaching.

    Living Space: A Home That Inspires

    My dream home would be a space that nurtures creativity and relaxation:

    • Spacious, with plenty of room for hosting friends
    • A dedicated home office for those work days
    • A killer outdoor setup for grilling and chilling
    • A pool for daily swims and relaxation
    • High-end audio system for immersive music experiences
    • Iconic furniture pieces like an Eames chair and Noguchi lamps

    It's not just about luxury, but about creating an environment that fuels my passions and supports my lifestyle.

    Activities: Pursuing Passions

    With more time and resources, I'd dive deeper into the activities that bring me joy:

    Pottery

    • Regular sessions to hone my craft
    • Potentially pursuing an apprenticeship or hosting a show
    • Exploring hand-building techniques
    • Creating functional art, like fish-serving plates

    Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu

    • Low-intensity training 2-3 times a week
    • Focus on technique and enjoyment rather than competition
    • Staying injury-free and maintaining fitness

    Swimming and Spearfishing

    • Daily swims in my home pool for fitness
    • Occasional spearfishing trips to connect with nature
    • Learning to prepare the fish I catch

    Travel: Expanding Horizons

    With financial freedom, I'd approach travel more intentionally:

    • Quarterly cross-country trips (2 weeks each)
    • Annual international trip to Europe or Japan
    • Two freediving trips per year, possibly swimming with whales or orcas

    The key is balancing adventure with my home-based passions and work commitments.

    Lifestyle: Quality Over Quantity

    Money can't buy happiness, but it can certainly improve quality of life:

    • High-quality, varied meals (possibly with a personal chef)
    • Regular entertainment (monthly jazz or comedy shows)
    • Hosting friends for dinners and gatherings
    • House cleaner and personal assistant for support

    The goal is to create a life rich in experiences and connections, not just possessions.

    Personal Growth: Never Stop Learning

    Even with financial security, I'd continue investing in myself:

    • Educating myself on investing and financial management
    • Being more selective with commitments and relationships
    • Focusing on "hell yes" experiences and friendships
    • Writing more, both for personal growth and to share insights

    Reflections on This Exercise

    This thought experiment isn't about daydreaming of wealth. It's about understanding what truly matters to me and how I can start incorporating elements of this ideal life into my current reality.

    Some key takeaways:

    1. Balance is crucial - even with unlimited resources, I'd want a mix of work, play, and personal growth.
    2. Experiences and relationships matter more than possessions.
    3. Health and wellness are non-negotiable, regardless of wealth.
    4. Creativity and learning remain central to my ideal life.

    While I may not have $20 million, this exercise has clarified my priorities and given me a roadmap for the life I want to create. It's a reminder that many aspects of our "ideal" life are within reach if we're intentional about our choices.

    What would your $20 million life look like? More importantly, what elements of that life can you start incorporating today?
